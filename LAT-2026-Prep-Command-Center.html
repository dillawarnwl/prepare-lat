Total target ≈ 89–91. Notice the subjective section (Essay + PS = 25 marks) is a quarter of the paper and is 100% controllable with a memorized skeleton. Students who chase MCQs and wing the writing cap out at ~75. You won't.

Priority order (by marks × predictability)

  1. Subjective writing (25 marks)
    Essay 15 + PS 10. Highest single block, fully templated. Lock the skeletons in week 1, polish in week 3.
  2. English (20 marks)
    Prepositions, synonyms/antonyms, sentence correction. Rule-based + the same ~50 vocab words recur.
  3. General Knowledge (20 marks)
    Geographic superlatives, SAARC, UN/world-org HQs, science firsts. Flashcard territory.
  4. Islamiat + Pak Studies (20 marks)
    Ghazawat with Hijri years, pillars, Quran facts; 1857–1947 timeline + 4 constitutions + geography.
  5. Urdu (10 marks)
    Muhawarat / zarb-ul-amsal, antonyms (متضاد), singular-plural (واحد/جمع). The same proverbs cycle through papers.
  6. Math (5 marks)
    Percentage, profit/loss, DST, averages, simple algebra. For you this is a warm-up — do it last in the hall so it never eats time.

Exam mechanics (don't lose marks to the clock)

Part I = 75 MCQs in 100 minutes → ~75 sec/MCQ. Do English + Math + the facts you're sure of first; flag-and-return on the rest. No negative marking — never leave a blank.
Part II = Essay + PS in 40 minutes. Budget 22 min essay / 16 min PS / 2 min to re-read. Max 200 words each — quality of structure beats length.
Write in your stronger language (English or Urdu) — examiner grades clarity, not which language. Never mix Roman Urdu / "khichdi".
Bring original CNIC/B-form + roll-number slip (issued ~11 June on the ETC portal). No calculator allowed.

03Essay + Personal Statement

25 of your 100 marks — and the only part you can fully script in advance. HEC's analysis of every paper since 2018 shows ~90% of essay prompts fall into three buckets: Social/Economic issues, Education, and Technology. Learn one template per bucket and you walk in unsurprised. Max 200 words each; ~22 min essay, ~16 min PS.

The 4-paragraph structure

1
Introduction — Hook + thesis. Name the topic and state the one position you'll defend. (2-3 sentences)
2
Body 1 — case / causes. Strongest argument or main causes, each tied to a concrete, ideally Pakistan-specific example.
3
Body 2 — counter-view / effects + solution. Acknowledge the other side or list effects, then give a realistic solution. This lifts a 9 to a 13.
4
Conclusion — Restate the thesis in fresh words + one forward line. No new points.

3 master templates (fill the blanks)

Memorize these three openings + closings; they cover all 20 expected topics.

A · Negative-issue template (corruption, inflation, poverty, cyberbullying, AI threats)

Open: "Among the many challenges facing Pakistan today, [ISSUE] stands out as one of the most pressing. It touches every section of society and, if left unchecked, threatens our social and economic stability. This essay examines its main causes and the steps needed to overcome it."

Close: "In conclusion, [ISSUE] is not beyond solution. With honest governance, public awareness, and collective effort, Pakistan can turn this challenge into a turning point for progress."

B · Positive / general template (education, justice, role of youth, literacy, teachers)

Open: "Few things matter more to the future of a nation than [TOPIC]. For a developing country like Pakistan, it is not a luxury but a necessity that shapes our progress and identity. This essay argues why [TOPIC] deserves to be a national priority."

Close: "Ultimately, investing in [TOPIC] is investing in Pakistan itself. If pursued sincerely, it will not only solve present problems but build the stronger nation we all hope to see."

C · Technology template (social media, AI, internet, mobile phones, Digital Pakistan)

Open: "In the 21st century, [TECH] has transformed the way we live, work, and think. Like any powerful tool, it is a double-edged sword — a source of great opportunity and, at the same time, serious risk. This essay weighs both sides and argues for balance."

Close: "In short, [TECH] is here to stay. The answer lies not in rejecting it but in using it wisely — harnessing its benefits while guarding against its harms."

Worked example 1 — Negative issue (~190 words)

Topic: "Impact of Inflation on the Common Man"

Among the many challenges facing Pakistan today, inflation stands out as one of the most pressing. It silently erodes the earnings of ordinary people and, if left unchecked, threatens the stability of millions of households.

The heaviest burden falls on the common man. When the prices of flour, fuel, and electricity rise faster than wages, a daily-wage worker or a salaried clerk must cut back on food, medicine, and his children's education. What was once affordable becomes a luxury, and families that were already struggling are pushed toward debt.

The causes are partly global and partly our own — a weak currency, heavy borrowing, and dependence on imports. Yet the solution lies within reach: disciplined economic policy, support for local production, targeted subsidies for the poorest, and an end to the hoarding that worsens shortages.

In conclusion, inflation is not beyond solution. With honest governance and collective effort, Pakistan can ease the pressure on its people and turn this hardship into a turning point for reform.

Worked example 2 — Technology (~185 words)

Topic: "Impact of Artificial Intelligence on Jobs"

In the 21st century, artificial intelligence has transformed the way we live and work. Like any powerful tool, it is a double-edged sword — a source of great opportunity and, at the same time, serious risk to the world of work.

On one hand, AI creates new possibilities. It removes dull, repetitive tasks, raises productivity, and opens entirely new careers in data, software, and digital services — fields in which young Pakistanis are already earning through freelancing and IT exports.

On the other hand, the same technology can displace workers whose jobs are easily automated, from clerks to factory hands. Without preparation, this could widen unemployment and inequality in a country that already struggles to create enough jobs.

The way forward is not fear but readiness. By reforming education toward technical and digital skills, and by training workers to use AI rather than compete with it, Pakistan can make the technology an ally.

In short, AI is here to stay. The answer lies not in rejecting it but in using it wisely — harnessing its benefits while guarding against its harms.
Why these score: clear thesis, one Pakistan-specific example, a counterweight + concrete solution, tidy conclusion — all under 200 words, no rare vocabulary needed.

04Personal Statement — 10 marks

An essay is about the topic; a PS is about you — your motivation, mindset, and goals. Use a 3-paragraph structure, never one block. Most prompts reduce to "Why law?" or "How will you contribute?" — prepare one strong answer and adapt it.

The 3-paragraph blueprint

1
Conviction + connection. A strong belief, then your personal link to it. "I have always believed… / Growing up, I saw…"
2
Evidence (longest). A personal example, skill, or observation that proves you care — and how law lets you act on it.
3
Goal + give-back. What you'll do with the degree and how it serves others. End forward-looking.

Worked example (~170 words)

Prompt: "Why do you want to study law, and how can you contribute to society as a lawyer?"

I have always believed that a society is only as fair as the access its weakest members have to justice. Coming from a background in computer science, I learned to think in systems and rules — and I came to see the law as the most powerful system of all, the one that decides how fairly every other system runs.

My analytical training taught me to read problems carefully, weigh evidence, and argue from logic rather than emotion — exactly the habits a good lawyer needs. I have often found myself helping friends and family make sense of documents and disputes they found intimidating. Law would let me turn that instinct into real, qualified help.

As a lawyer, I want to make legal understanding less of a privilege and more of a right — through honest practice and, eventually, pro-bono work for those who cannot afford counsel. My goal is not only to practise law, but to widen the door to justice for those standing outside it.
PS killers: generic "lawyers are important" lines (that's an essay), no personal example, exceeding 200 words, or sounding interchangeable with everyone else. Make at least one sentence unmistakably yours.

IN / ON / AT quick logic

AT — precise point: at 5 o'clock, at the door, at night.

ON — surface / specific day or date: on the table, on Monday, on 14 August.

IN — enclosed / longer period: in the room, in June, in 2026, in the morning.

Confusing pairs (the traps)

PairRule
Between / AmongBetween = TWO; Among = THREE+ (a group).
Beside / BesidesBeside = next to (place); Besides = in addition to.
During / WhileDuring + noun (during the flight); While + verb (while travelling).
Fewer / LessFewer = countable (fewer books); Less = uncountable (less water).
Its / It'sIts = possessive; It's = 'it is'.
Lie / LayLie = recline (no object); Lay = put down (needs an object).
Advice / AdviseAdvice = noun; Advise = verb.
Affect / EffectAffect = verb (to influence); Effect = noun (a result).

Sentence-correction rules that recur

1. Subject-verb agreement: a singular subject takes a singular verb. One of the boys is absent.

2. 'Either...or / Neither...nor': verb agrees with the nearer subject. Neither he nor his friends were there.

3. After 'did / does / do', use the base verb. He did not go (not 'went').

4. 'Lest' is followed by 'should'. Work hard lest you should fail.

5. Two singular nouns joined by 'and' take a plural verb; if they refer to one thing, singular. Bread and butter is my breakfast.
